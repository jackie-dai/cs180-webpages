Project 3: Morphing

Jackie Dai - Fall 2024

Abstract

This project explores the ins and outs of morphing. In order to execute a morph we needed to triangulate two images. This way we can find warp an average geometry and cross dissolve the colors to produce a morphing effect.

Part 1: Defining Correspondences

To begin our morphing journey, I picked out these two gorgeous photos of Barack Obama and Gary Drinkard.

Putting these images through a student made tool, I defined correspondence points for both images and loaded the points into python where I called scipy.spatial.delaunay to triangulate the points. Below is the triangulation visualized

Part 2: Computing the “Mid-way Face”

To prepare for the next task, I implemented one step of the morphing sequence, the “mid-way face”. This first involved averaging the correspondence points between the two images to get the average shape. Here I used warp_frac=0.5 .

My next task was to fill in the color of the midway face sampled from img1. But first, I had to calculate an affine transformation between the img1 and mid_face so that I could find an inverse transformation. The inverse transformation is important because I need to use it to warp from coordinates on the mid_face to img1. I found these warped coordinates from, coordinates I found on the mid face using skimage.draw.polygon.


However, by warping the coordinates from the midface to source, I would get coordinates in between points in floats. Meaning that I had to interpolate the nearby pixel values to sample a color. My initial approach to filling in each triangle of the midway face was by using
scipy.interpolate.griddata(points, values, xi, method="nearest") . Points were the source coordinates and values are the color pixel values associated to each coordinate. xi are which points to interpolate. At first, I just passed in an empty 2d array of coordinates for the whole image. This was extremely costly, taking ~6 minutes to generate one image. I knew this was not viable as I needed to generate 45 frames to produce my morphing sequence. Which would like 6 * 45 minutes.

So I optimized my approach by passing in only coordinates of the triangle I was currently filling in. This cut my runtime for generating one image to ~9 seconds. Finally, I repeated the same process above with img2 and the midway face, blending the two outputs together with dissolve_frac=0.5

Part 3: The Morph Sequence

To create the morph animation, I simply interpolated my warp_frac and wrap_dissolve from 0 to 1 and generated a total of 45 frames to produce a smooth morphing sequence from Obama to Gary.
