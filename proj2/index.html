Introduction

In this project we explored 2d convolutions, gaussian filters, and utilized these tools to manipulate image’s frequencies to achieve cool results such as image sharpening, hybrid images, and image blending!

1.1 Finite Difference Operator

To show the partial derivatives of the cameraman image, I picked two finite operators dx=np.array([[1,-1]]) and dy=np.array([[1], [-1]]). I performed a 2d convolution between the image and derivatives in the x and y direction.

Next, I found the gradient magnitude by summing the squares of dx and dy and taking the square root. Finally, I binarized the image to better visualize the edges by applying a threshold of alpha=0.15

1.2 Derivative of Gaussian Filter

The images produced above were quite noisy, so here I applied a gaussian blur with scipy’s gaussian kernal. This smoothed out the edges and produced a smoother result. For efficiency reasons, I convolved the gaussian kernal with dx and dy so that I can apply it to any image with one convolution.

2.1 Image “Sharpening”

Some images look a bit plain. We can sharpen the details/edges by taking the low frequencies (convolving with gaussian kernal) and subtracting those away from the the original image. This makes the high frequencies more prominent and the result of this are sharper edges

2.3 Gaussian and Laplacian Stack

To prepare for image blending we first created a gaussian stack. A gaussian stack has n layers, the first layer starting with the original image. The next layer is made by applying a gaussian filter to the previous layer and so on. I started with n=5 layers, a kernal size k=10, and gaussian std sigma=3.

We end up with a sequence of images more blurry than the previous:

Next, we extracted the mid bands from the images by taking the difference between a layer and the next layer. Stacking the results, we ended up with a laplacian stack.

Then we summed up the laplacian stack and added the lowest frequency image from the gaussian stack

2.4 Multi Resolution Blending

To blend two images, we made gaussian and laplacian stacks for both images. Next I created a vertical mask and created a gaussian stack for the mask as well.

I iterated through each layer of the laplacian stack and performed this operation s(i,j) = (gr_m * l_img1) + (1-gr_m * l_img2) to get a splined/merged result. Additionally, I took the lowest frequency in the gaussian stack for each image, masked each blurry image, merged and appended the result to the stack. Finally, I took the merged result of each layer, summed, and min-max normalized it to get the final image: The Orapple.
